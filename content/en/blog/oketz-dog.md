It was another intense day of combat on the Gaza border. The clock showed 19:20 when the red phone in the command center rang. The report was brief and familiar: 'Shrapnel casualties, urgent evacuation required.'

Within less than a minute, the medical team under the battalion doctor's command was already on the ambulance, heading to the rendezvous point. On the way, the information began to clarify — this wasn't just about an injured soldier, but a more complex event.

## The Surprise in the Field

When the forces linked up, the sight was difficult. Alongside a soldier hit by shrapnel in his limbs, another casualty lay on the stretcher, breathing heavily and covered in blood. It was Pumba, a combat dog from the Oketz (K-9) unit.

Pumba suffered severe head, neck, and ear injuries. He was in a clouded state of consciousness, barely responding to his surroundings, and his breathing was rapid and shallow.

## The Dilemma and the Decision

In a normal situation, protocols are clear — treat humans. But at that moment, in the field, the doctor understood there was no time for protocols. 'I realized that if we didn't provide life-saving treatment right here and now, we'd lose him within minutes,' he recalls.

Simultaneously with treating the wounded soldier, the medical team — which included paramedics and medics from the battalion — rallied for the extraordinary mission. They performed procedures to stop Pumba's massive bleeding, stabilized his respiratory condition, and fought for him exactly as they would fight for any other team member.

## The Happy Ending

The determination paid off. The bleeding stopped, and Pumba was evacuated for further treatment at the veterinary hospital in Beit Dagan. The rehabilitation was long, but Pumba walked again.

For us, Pumba's story is a reminder of the essence of our role as reserve medical teams: to save lives, to maintain humanity within the chaos, and to never give up on any fighter — whether they walk on two legs or four.
