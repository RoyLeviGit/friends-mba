1969 — we were discharged from the IDF, a group of medics from the Paratroop Nahal brigade. In 1970, a training exercise to establish a "paratrooper platoon" in the battalion. Another one of the founder's bold ideas. Years of attachments as medics to various units followed.

## Saturday, Yom Kippur 1973

Sirens pierce the air. We walk out of the synagogue. A phone call from the unit — "Get to Tel HaShomer."

I pack a small bag and arrive at the military personnel center. Chaos reigns, as expected. Nobody knows anyone, no organic units. Minimal equipment: an Uzi and two magazines, a World War-era helmet, a blanket — and that's it. By midnight, we're already on a sputtering "Gaz" truck, with equipment crates and 7 other medics.

## Into the Fire

Two trucks set out. At dawn, God knows where, we're dropped off the truck and receive cheerful news: "The Egyptian commando is coming for you." Uzi at the ready. A ball of fire passes in front of us — a missile fired at our forces. The commando apparently got scared off by "our force" and retreated.

We attach to a 175mm artillery battalion and spend about two weeks in a cycle of "we shell until they find our position, then they shell us." We serve as the battalion aid station, treating the wounded, until the crossing to the other side of the canal on the Spider axis.

Turns out we were the most combat-engaged element in the battalion.

## The Return

After 14 days cut off from the battalion, they find us and we receive orders to report to a field hospital in Refidim. After a night helping at the hospital, we're picked up and head to Tzrifin for a brief refresher. Then back down to Sinai, in various formations until the end of the war — about 6 months, rotating through all kinds of units.

From there on — until age 51, we kept serving.
