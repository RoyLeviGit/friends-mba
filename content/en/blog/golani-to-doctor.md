An extraordinary story of a Golani soldier who was severely wounded, survived, decided to study medicine — and today serves as a surgeon and doctor in our battalion. Recently, he returned to the ward where he was hospitalized to encourage new patients.

## From the Battlefield to the Operating Room

One of our doctors, now a pediatric surgeon serving in the battalion, began his military journey as a fighter in Golani Brigade's Battalion 13. In 2009, after only about 10 months in the army, he was severely wounded in his right hand during Operation Cast Lead.

The serious injury led to a prolonged hospitalization of approximately 3.5 months in the hand surgery department at Sheba Medical Center. He then underwent a long rehabilitation period lasting about two and a half years, during which he also chose to volunteer and work with at-risk youth.

Out of the injury and rehabilitation, he made a life-changing decision: to study medicine. He completed his medical studies at the Technion, became a pediatric surgeon, and today we are proud that he is part of our family and serves as a doctor in the battalion.

## "Proof That With Willpower, Recovery Is Possible"

14 years after that injury, he closed a deeply moving circle. He returned to the same ward at Sheba where he was treated, this time as a surgeon, to embrace, encourage, and strengthen the new patients and wounded.

The head nurse of the hand surgery department at Sheba shared her excitement from the encounter:

"Today I was moved. A soldier who was wounded in Cast Lead 14 years ago and was hospitalized in the hand ward for about 3.5 months came back today to encourage and embrace the new patients and serve as an example that with willpower, recovery is possible. It was immensely gratifying to have treated him, and it's a great source of pride to see him today healthy and a surgeon no less! And to know that we played a part in his recovery!"

## Coming Back to Life

This story represents the spirit of our medical personnel. These days, we see many rehabilitated soldiers returning to new patients to encourage and strengthen them on their path to recovery. We at the Friends of the Medical Battalion Association are moved by each and every one of them.
